🎣 What is Phishing?

Think of phishing as a digital costume party where criminals dress up as someone you trust—like your bank, a delivery service, or a social media site. Their goal is to trick you into handing over the keys to your digital life: passwords, financial details, and personal information. They don't just want your data; they want to use it to access your accounts, steal your money, or commit identity theft.

These scams are effective because they exploit human emotions. By creating a sense of urgency ("Your account will be deleted!") or curiosity ("You've won a prize!"), they pressure you into making quick decisions without careful thought. Common forms include email phishing, targeted "spear phishing," SMS-based "smishing," and voice-based "vishing."

💡 Quick Tips to Spot a Phish

✅ Hover Before You Click

Before your cursor even thinks about clicking, let it rest over the link for a moment. A small pop-up will reveal the link's true destination. If the URL looks suspicious or doesn't match the sender, it's a trap. On mobile, a long press on the link will achieve the same result.

✅ Analyze the Emotion

Scammers are masters of emotional manipulation. If an email makes you feel intense urgency, fear, greed, or even extreme curiosity, stop. Legitimate organizations rarely communicate with such high stakes. This is a tactic to make you bypass your logical brain.

✅ Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Think of 2FA as a second lock on your digital door. Even if a scammer steals your password (the first key), they still can't get in without the second key—a code sent only to your phone or authentication app. It's one of the single most effective defenses you can use.

✅ Scrutinize for Imperfections

While AI is making scams better, many still contain mistakes. Look for poor grammar, awkward phrasing, or spelling errors. Check the sender's email address for subtle misspellings (e.g., support@micros0ft.com). These are tell-tale signs of a fake.

*Current Trend: QR code phishing ("quishing") is on the rise. Scammers place fake QR codes in emails or even on public posters. When scanned, they can lead to malicious sites or install malware. Always consider the source before you scan a QR code.
